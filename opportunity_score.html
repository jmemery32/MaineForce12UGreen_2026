What This Measures

Weighted innings played by position across the full season. Each position carries a weight reflecting how much active development it provides — pitcher in every play, left fielder may not touch the ball for an inning. The score captures meaningful fielding development time per player.

Position Weights

C2.5Constant engagement, field general
SS2.0Most demanding infield spot
2B / 3B / CF1.5Active infield / premier OF
1B1.2Infield, more passive
LF / RF1.0Baseline outfield

Raw vs. Rate

Raw Score = Σ (innings × weight). Reflects absolute development time.

Opportunity Rate = Raw ÷ (GP × 6). Normalizes for missed games — the fairer comparison.

Coaching Goal

Well-distributed teams see most players 0.9–1.3 on Opp Rate. Below 0.8 signals a kid needing more high-value innings. Above 1.4 reflects heavy investment in premium spots (P/SS/C).

What This Measures

Batting order position determines how many plate appearances a player gets. A leadoff hitter bats ~20–25% more often than cleanup in a short game. This score captures the offensive opportunity each player received through lineup placement.

Formula

For each game, lineup spot is normalized 0–1:

Spot Score = (Roster Size − Pos) ÷ (Roster Size − 1)

1.0 = leadoff. 0.0 = last in order. Averaged across all games.

Data Source

Batting orders drawn from all game plan HTML files: Apr 26 (G1/G2), May 3, May 10, May 17, May 23 pool play, bracket, and tournament games — 20 planned lineups total.

Coaching Goal

Pure rotation = 0.50 for all. Above 0.60 = consistent top-of-order. Below 0.35 = bottom-of-order. Intentional based on ability, but worth reviewing for players who earned more.

Combined Score

Blends Defensive Opportunity Rate and Offensive Opportunity Score 50/50. Both scores are normalized to comparable scales. Shows players who received holistic opportunity — premium fielding spots and productive lineup positions.

How to Read It

Above 0.90 = high opportunity across both dimensions.

0.65–0.90 = solid, well-rounded opportunity.

Below 0.65 = worth revisiting this player's role for next season.

What This Measures

How closely actual season fielding statistics matched what was drawn up in the game plan HTML files. For each player, planned innings at each position are compared to actual innings. Overlap = adherence.

Method

For each position: compute fraction of planned vs. actual innings. Adherence = sum of min(planned%, actual%) across 9 positions — the "intersection" of the two distributions. 100% = executed exactly as planned.

What Lowers It

In-game adjustments, injured arms, absent players, and defensive subs all cause deviations. Lower score ≠ bad coaching — it means the game demanded flexibility.

Season Summary

Overall weighted adherence across all 13 players: 84%. Roughly 5 out of every 6 fielding innings went exactly as planned.

OPS = on-base + slugging. Combined opportunity = defensive rate + offensive score (equal weight).

What This Measures

Whether each player's combined opportunity was proportional to their performance. Compares OPS rank (offensive ability), ERA rank (pitching ability), and combined opportunity rank. A "fair" assignment means high performers got high opportunity and vice versa.

Verdict Definitions

Earned — opportunity closely matches performance.

Balanced — minor gap; explainable by roster/role constraints.

Under-served — performed above their opportunity level.

Over-served — received more opportunity than performance alone justified.

Key Finding

Season overall opportunity distribution was defensible and largely tied to performance. The clearest outlier: Griffin Strattard — 2nd-highest OPS, best QAB%, but consistently batted 6th–7th and played almost exclusively 1B. A role expansion candidate for next season.

Caveats

OPS alone doesn't capture defense, coachability, or positional fit. Some "over-served" players were given premium spots to develop, not because they'd already earned them. That's a coaching investment, not an error.
